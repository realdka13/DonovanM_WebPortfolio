Printed Circuit Board Counter

Objective

To learn ECAD design and manufacture my first printed circuit board

Overview

I have made many breadboard circuits and converted a few to perfboard circuits, but breadboards are prototyping tools and perfboard circuits are not the most compact or clean. Before this project, I had never used electrical computer aided design (ECAD) software or soldered surface mounted components. An easy circuit was chosen to keep the focus on the ECAD design and soldering instead of troubleshooting a circuit. The circuit chosen is 4-bit counter counting from 0 – 15 and displaying on both LEDs, and in hex on a 7-segment display.

Final Product

The final product is a 2 layer PCB featuring a CD74HC190 4-bit binary counter with 4 LEDs to view the output directly. The counter output is also routed to a SN74LS48N BCD to Seven Segment decoder which is then hooked up to a 7-segment display. A 3-pin screw terminal provides 5v power and a clock from a separate source. The counting speed is determined by the frequency of the supplied clock, ideally being 1 – 3 hz, otherwise you wouldnt be able to see the LEDs incriment. All components are through-hole except for the resistors. 7 jumper wires were required due to a design flaw on the board, which left some unconnected traces.

Conclusion

This project completed the objective, although not without a few mistakes. First, the ICs were soldered directly to the PCB. This is an issue as it makes it very difficult to replace a faulty IC, whether faulty from the factory or overheated when soldering. For future PCBs, if using DIP (dual in-line package) ICs, an IC socket should be soldered in that location and the IC placed inside the socket. Second, due to a misunderstanding of trace routing on 2-layer boards, a total of 7 pads were left unconnected. This could have been avoided by properly utilizing a design rule check, which was also missed. Future projects will follow stricter procedures as well as utilize a ground plane.
